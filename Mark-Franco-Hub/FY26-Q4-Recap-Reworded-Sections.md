## Rewritten Section 1 — Canadian Tire (Retail & Consumer Goods)

**Architecture Review Board Bot (ARBBot) — Solution Envisioning / Architecture Design — Mark Franco (lead), with Ansar Mohammed.**

- **Objective:** Move Canadian Tire from wall-to-wall M365 Copilot adoption (~50% active usage) into governed, low-code and pro-code enterprise agents.
- **Hub Approach:** Rather than co-building from scratch, Mark brought a **pre-built accelerator he had already developed** — a human-in-the-loop, multi-agent architecture-review assistant on Copilot Studio + Azure AI Foundry + MCP — and **deployed it directly into Canadian Tire's Foundry instance**, then iterated it with the customer across three sessions (Envisioning → Architecture Design → 2-day Rapid Prototype). This is the "arrive with repeatable IP" motion in action: reusable, deployable Hub assets that compress time-to-value.
- **Outcome:** The accelerator proved so effective that it has been **packaged as a reusable Hub accelerator and shared globally** — a direct example of one SE's IP scaling across the entire Hub network.

*Why the change:* The original wording read as if ARBBot was created fresh in the room. The more valuable — and more accurate — story is that Mark walked in with reusable IP and deployed it, which is exactly the repeatable-IP motion we've been driving toward all year.

## Rewritten Section 2 — Hydro One (Energy & Resources)

**AI transformation partnership — Mark Franco (Lead Hub Solution Engineer on all engagements), with Ansar Mohammed on the landing-zone and governance/security workstreams.**

- **Scope:** Not a single design session — a **sustained six-engagement partnership** that Mark led end-to-end across the fiscal year: Use Case Prioritization & Solution Envisioning, Architecture Design, Rapid Prototype, AI Business Envisioning (Governance & Security), Agentic AI Design Session, and Agentic AI Rapid Prototype.
- **Hub Approach:** Mark was the business sponsor's named point of contact — they came to the Hub specifically to work with him. He led the envisioning that **prioritized the use cases and surfaced the foundational (landing-zone) gaps**, which in turn scoped the platform-foundation and governance/security work that Ansar delivered. The use cases developed across these sessions include:
  - **Schematic IQ** — engineering/diagram intelligence
  - **HR Bot** — resume intake and recruiter applicant-search agent
  - **Advanced SharePoint Search** — enterprise search prototype
- **Outcome:** Delivered a working enterprise-search prototype — Hydro One's **third pro-code AI solution** — with the customer "super happy." Beyond the artifacts, the engagement rebuilt the customer's confidence in a Hub-led, foundation-first approach: their leadership came to understand *why* the recommended envisioning-first sequence mattered, and re-engaged repeatedly across the year.

*Why the change:* "An agentic AI design session" collapses six led engagements and multiple named use cases into one. Listing the use cases and the correct engagement count reflects the real scope. Ansar's landing-zone and governance/security contributions are real and credited — but they were scoped *from the gaps Mark identified while leading the envisioning*, so the sequencing of credit should reflect that.
